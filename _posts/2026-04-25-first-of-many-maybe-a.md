## What I read and reflections:

* This week, A book: *India is Broken: A People Betrayed, Independence to Today* by Ashoka Mody. 

<figure>
  <img src="https://spia.princeton.edu/sites/default/files/2023-09/Mody%2BBook%20Cover.png" alt="Ashoka Mody and the book cover">
  <figcaption>From Princeton School of Public Policy</figcaption>
</figure>

I liked this because it does something more than Ram Guha's *India After Gandhi*. Guha's book was amazing in giving a long-term narrative of political history since 1947. 

Mody does the same, but with political *and economic* history. He moves beyond just the politics to also look at the economy. One of the key takeaways of the book is captured here in a big-ish but really nice quote at the end of Chapter 23. 
> "At the start of this now seventy-five-year journey, India faced three challenges: breathe new life into the agricultural sector, provide good urban jobs, and compete in the international marketplace. At the end of this journey, India faces three challenges: breathe new life into an ailing
agricultural sector, provide many more jobs in an economy that seems chronically unable to create jobs, and compete in a much tougher international setting." 

This is quite an Interesting analysis of the post-independent India that really emphasizes quality of life. Really, at its core, economics is meant to study that, but sometimes we lose the larger picture in details of economic indicators. 

But what I loved the most about this book was really good graphs that capture and say a lot. I am attaching those here. They were mind-blowing for me 🤯.

There is a lot more about the book, but I thought these were interesting bits. It's quite readable so if you are looking for a book, I think it's a good one for anyone living in India who wants to understand why India is as it is. 

There is a lot more in the book which is interesting (my scholarly side can't stomach that I didn't give you a whole book length analysis of the book). But let's stop on *gyaan* there.
